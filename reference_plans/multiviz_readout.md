# Enterprise AI adoption — 2026 mid-year readout

Audience: the enterprise leadership team and the AI program office — the people who fund the AI program and need the half-year picture.
This is the full mid-year data pull. The deck pulls the charts that carry the story; not every number here lands on a slide.

Preferences:
- Tone: factual, measured — a readout, not a pitch.
- Sub-brand: base IBM.
- Length: six slides — cover, adoption two ways, where the value lands, pilot to production, cost against return, the mid-year read.

## Cover
- Enterprise AI adoption — 2026 mid-year readout.
- Subtitle: adoption, value, the pilot-to-production gap, and the cost curve — the half-year in charts.
- Scope: eight functions, five value categories, six quarters of trend.

## Adoption, two ways — the trend and the spread
Two views of adoption on one slide: how fast it grew, and where it is uneven.
The trend — share of teams using sanctioned AI tools, six quarters, Q1 2025 to Q2 2026: 22%, 31%, 42%, 54%, 63%, 71%.
The spread — adoption by business function, Q2 2026, share of teams in each function:
- Engineering — 88%
- Customer support — 74%
- Marketing — 69%
- Sales — 61%
- Operations — 58%
- Finance — 52%
- HR — 44%
- Legal — 31%
The read: adoption more than tripled in six quarters and is past 70% enterprise-wide — but the spread, Engineering at 88% to Legal at 31%, is the real story. The average hides a 57-point gap.

## Where the value lands — the chart and the number
The annualized value realized, by use-case category, and the one number it sums to.
Value by category, annualized Q2 2026 run-rate:
- Customer service automation — $16.5M
- Code generation and developer tools — $11.2M
- Document processing and extraction — $8.4M
- Marketing content and campaigns — $7.1M
- Forecasting and analytics — $4.8M
The headline: $48.0M in annualized value realized — 2.4x the pilot-year run-rate of $20M. Customer service automation alone is more than a third of it; the top two categories are 58% of the total. Value concentrates — the thesis held.

## From pilot to production — the funnel and the gap
How pilots convert, and where they stall.
The conversion funnel, cumulative since program start:
- Pilots launched — 72
- Reached production — 31
- Scaled enterprise-wide — 12
So 43% of pilots reach production and 17% scale; the drop from pilot to production is the gap that matters.
By function — pilots launched and how many reached production:
- Engineering — 14 launched, 9 in production
- Customer support — 11 launched, 7 in production
- Marketing — 12 launched, 5 in production
- Operations — 10 launched, 4 in production
- Sales — 9 launched, 3 in production
- Finance, HR and Legal combined — 16 launched, 3 in production
Engineering and Customer support convert at roughly 64%; everyone else is under 45%. The pilot-to-production gap is an enablement gap, not an idea gap.

## Cost against return — two cost views
What the program costs, and how that compares to what it returns.
Program cost, annualized, by category — $25.4M total:
- Model and API spend — $9.2M
- Platform and infrastructure — $5.1M
- Integration and build — $6.8M
- Enablement and training — $2.4M
- Governance and risk — $1.9M
Cost against return: $25.4M in, $48.0M out — a 1.9x return, up from 1.3x at the pilot-year mark. Model and API spend is the largest line and the fastest-growing, up 34% half-on-half; integration and build is falling as the platform replaces one-off work.

## The mid-year read — three things the numbers say
- Adoption is broad but uneven — 71% enterprise-wide, a 57-point spread; the next push is the laggard functions, not the leaders.
- Value is real and concentrating — $48M annualized, 2.4x the pilot year, 58% of it in two categories.
- The cost curve is bending the right way — 1.9x return, integration spend falling as the platform scales; model spend is the line to watch.
